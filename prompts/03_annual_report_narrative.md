# Prompt 3 — Annual Report Narrative & Impact Story System

## Use This When
You need to write the narrative content for your annual report — the executive summary, year-in-review story arc, impact stories, board chair letter, donor recognition copy, and the statistical callouts that make funders and donors feel the year's progress.

## What You'll Get
- Annual report narrative arc (800-1,200 words): executive summary + year in review + financials story + forward look
- 3 beneficiary impact stories using CARS framework (300 words each)
- Board chair letter (250 words)
- Donor recognition tier descriptions (5 giving levels)
- 8 infographic headline statistics (formatted for design)
- Planned giving introduction paragraph

---

## The Prompt

```
You are an expert nonprofit communications director who writes annual reports that raise money, not just fulfill reporting requirements. Write the complete narrative content for an annual report using these inputs:

**ORGANIZATION INFO:**
- Name: [ORGANIZATION NAME]
- Mission (1 sentence): [MISSION]
- Fiscal year covered: [YEAR, e.g., "FY 2025 (July 1, 2024 – June 30, 2025)"]
- Geographic service area: [CITY/REGION]

**PROGRAM DATA (provide for each of your 3 main programs):**

Program 1:
- Name: [PROGRAM NAME]
- What it does: [1-2 sentences]
- Key metric: [e.g., "served 1,200 people" or "provided 45,000 meals"]
- Year-over-year change: [e.g., "+18% vs prior year" or "stable"]

Program 2:
- Name: [PROGRAM NAME]
- What it does: [1-2 sentences]
- Key metric: [METRIC]
- Year-over-year change: [CHANGE]

Program 3:
- Name: [PROGRAM NAME]
- What it does: [1-2 sentences]
- Key metric: [METRIC]
- Year-over-year change: [CHANGE]

**FINANCIAL DATA:**
- Total revenue: $[AMOUNT]
- % to programs: [e.g., "82%"]
- % to administration: [e.g., "10%"]
- % to fundraising: [e.g., "8%"]
- Largest revenue source: [e.g., "government grants (43%)"]
- Endowment / reserve status: [e.g., "6-month reserve" or "no endowment"]
- Year-over-year donation growth: [e.g., "+12%" or "-5% with explanation"]

**BENEFICIARY STORIES (3 raw stories — provide names and details, or leave names generic):**

Story 1:
- Person/family: [NAME / "a family we served"]
- Challenge before: [SITUATION]
- What the org did: [INTERVENTION]
- Result: [OUTCOME, with metric if possible]

Story 2: [Same format]

Story 3: [Same format]

**LEADERSHIP:**
- Executive director name and years in role: [NAME, YEARS]
- Board chair name and professional background: [NAME, TITLE/COMPANY]
- Board size: [NUMBER]
- Any leadership changes this year: [YES/NO + details if yes]

**GIVING TIERS (provide 5 named tiers with dollar ranges):**
- Tier 1: [NAME] — $[RANGE]
- Tier 2: [NAME] — $[RANGE]
- Tier 3: [NAME] — $[RANGE]
- Tier 4: [NAME] — $[RANGE]
- Tier 5: [NAME] — $[RANGE, e.g., "$10,000+"]

**FORWARD LOOK:**
- Top priority for next year: [GOAL/PROJECT]
- Funding target for that priority: $[AMOUNT]

---

DELIVER:

## SECTION 1: ANNUAL REPORT NARRATIVE ARC (800-1,200 words)

Structure:

**Opening (Executive Summary equivalent — 150-200 words):**
Lead with the year's single most powerful impact statement. Not "it was another great year for [Org]" — lead with the result. ("This year, 1,200 children in Henderson ate breakfast on a Tuesday in July. That sounds ordinary. In our city, for the families we serve, it wasn't.")

Then: mission reminder → year's headline number → the challenge that remains.

**Year in Review (400-500 words):**
Three sections, one per program:
- Each section: program name → 1-sentence what it does → key metric → 1-sentence impact sentence → year-over-year progress note
- Connect the programs to each other: "Our meals program gets children to school; our tutoring program keeps them there; our family counseling program means they have a home to come home to."

**Financials Story (150-200 words):**
Don't just restate the numbers — tell the story of the numbers.
- Lead with the program efficiency ratio: "82 cents of every dollar you gave went directly to the people we serve."
- Acknowledge the revenue source mix without making it sound fragile
- Year-over-year donation growth framing (positive or honest)
- Reserve status: responsible stewardship framing

**Forward Look (150-200 words):**
- The unmet need: what remains to be done
- Next year's top priority
- Funding target with a specific what-it-will-do framing
- Invitation to continue the partnership

---

## SECTION 2: IMPACT STORIES (3 stories, 300 words each)

Use the CARS framework for each:
- **C — Context:** Who is this person/family? What was their situation before? (Don't make it gratuitous — enough for empathy, not pity.)
- **A — Action:** What specifically did your organization do? (Name the program, the person who helped, the intervention.)
- **R — Result:** What changed? Specific metric or observable outcome.
- **S — So What:** Why does this story matter beyond this one person? What does it show about the need in your community?

Each story should:
- Open with a scene, not a summary ("It was a Wednesday in February when Maria walked into our resource center for the first time...")
- Include a quote (real or illustrative — note "quote paraphrased for privacy" if needed)
- End with a forward-looking sentence (what this person's future looks like, or what the org is doing next)
- Be 100% donor-ready — no jargon, no acronyms, no program codes

---

## SECTION 3: BOARD CHAIR LETTER (250 words)

Structure:
1. Personal opening (1-2 sentences): why this person serves on this board
2. Year's headline achievement (1 statistic)
3. Organizational health note (board engagement, reserves, governance)
4. Forward look: the board's commitment to next year's priority
5. Specific thank-you: to staff, volunteers, donors — in that order
6. Closing: direct, personal, not boilerplate

Tone: Credible, grateful, forward-looking. The board chair letter is read by major donors and funders — it signals organizational stability, not just gratitude.

---

## SECTION 4: DONOR RECOGNITION TIER DESCRIPTIONS (5 tiers)

For each giving tier, write:
- Tier name (use the one provided)
- Dollar range confirmation
- 2-3 sentences: what this giving level means in impact terms ("Your gift at this level provides...")
- Any recognition benefits (naming rights, event invitations, etc. — use standard if none specified)

Format these as they'd appear in the annual report donor roll — scannable, dignified, specific.

---

## SECTION 5: INFOGRAPHIC HEADLINE STATISTICS (8 stats)

Format each as: [BIG NUMBER] / [SHORT LABEL] / [ONE-SENTENCE CONTEXT]

Pull the most powerful data points from the inputs provided. Include:
- Primary output metric (meals served, people housed, students tutored, etc.)
- Year-over-year growth stat
- Efficiency ratio (% to programs)
- Volunteer hours
- Number of donors / donor growth
- Geographic reach
- 2 more from the program data provided

Each stat should be a 3-element package:
- BIG NUMBER: [formatted cleanly — "45,000" not "45000"; "$2.1M" not "$2,100,000"]
- SHORT LABEL: [3-6 words — "meals served last year" / "of donations go to programs"]
- CONTEXT: [1 sentence — "That's 18% more than the year before, serving families across all 9 Henderson zip codes."]

---

## SECTION 6: PLANNED GIVING INTRODUCTION (1 paragraph, 100-125 words)

Write a single paragraph introducing the concept of legacy / planned giving to donors reading the annual report.

Guidelines:
- No jargon ("bequest", "testamentary gift" — use plain language)
- No pressure — this is an awareness plant, not an ask
- Frame it as a way to continue the mission beyond their lifetime
- Include a contact name for follow-up conversation
- Close with a low-barrier next step ("Download our guide to legacy giving at [URL] or call [NAME] at [PHONE]")
```

## Example Output (Excerpt)

**Organization:** Desert Hope Community Resource Center, Henderson NV

---

**Annual Report Opening:**

> This year, 1,247 children in Henderson ate a hot meal on a Tuesday in July.
>
> That sounds like an ordinary thing. In the neighborhoods we serve — where summer means no school cafeteria, where a parent might be working two jobs and still coming up short — it wasn't ordinary at all. It was the difference between a child who went back to school in August ready to learn, and one who didn't.
>
> For 23 years, Desert Hope has filled the gaps that government programs leave behind. Last year, we served 8,400 families across Henderson and North Las Vegas — more than any year in our history. We did it with 340 volunteers, 42 staff members, and the generosity of 3,100 donors who believe that a neighbor in need is a community's responsibility.
>
> The need has not gone away. If anything, it's grown. Here's what we accomplished together — and where we're going next.

---

**CARS Impact Story — Maria's Family:**

> On a Wednesday in February, Maria walked into our family resource center carrying two trash bags.
>
> Inside were her 7-year-old twins' school clothes, a week's worth of canned goods, and a folder of eviction papers. Her husband had been laid off three months earlier. The rental assistance she'd applied for hadn't come through. She had 12 days.
>
> Desert Hope's housing navigator, James, sat with Maria for two hours that afternoon. He identified a state rental assistance program she hadn't known about, connected her with a local legal aid attorney who reviewed her eviction notice, and enrolled her twins in our after-school meals program so she'd have one fewer thing to worry about.
>
> Sixty days later, Maria's family was still in their apartment.
>
> "I didn't know places like this existed," she told us. "I thought I had to figure it out alone."
>
> Families like Maria's are why Desert Hope exists. Last year, our housing navigation program kept 312 families in their homes. The waiting list has 87 more.

## Pro Tips

- **Annual reports are read by major donors, foundation officers, and government funders** — not just supporters. Write at that level of credibility.
- **The financials story is your trust signal.** 82 cents on the dollar to programs is better than most — say so explicitly. If your ratio is lower, explain why (capital campaign, building year, etc.) rather than burying it.
- **CARS framework prevents the common mistake:** most nonprofits lead with the intervention ("we provided meals"), not the person ("Marcus almost didn't eat"). Donors give to people, not programs.
- **The planned giving paragraph plants a seed.** You won't see results for 5-10 years — which is exactly why you need to plant it every year.
